This section details the methodology for classifying AI systems based on risk levels. It ensures alignment with the EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, ISO/IEC 23894, and other global standards.
________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________
# 1. Purpose
The purpose of this framework is to identify, classify, and manage AI systems based on their potential risk to health, safety, fundamental rights, and organizational reputation.
<br />**EU AI Act – High-Risk Definition:** "High-risk AI systems are those which, by virtue of their intended purpose, pose a high risk of harm to the health, safety or fundamental rights of natural persons." (EU AI Act, Title III)
<br />**NIST AI RMF – Risk:** "AI risk is a composite measure of an AI system’s potential for causing harm and the likelihood that the harm will occur." (NIST AI RMF 1.0)
<br />**ISO/IEC 23894 – Risk Management:** "Risk assessment shall consider intended and reasonably foreseeable misuse of AI systems." (ISO/IEC 23894:2023)

# 2. Risk Levels
## Low-Risk AI Systems
-	Minimal impact on individual rights or organizational operations
-	Examples: AI tools for internal reporting, non-sensitive data analytics
-	Approval: Simplified internal approval (CAIO + System Owner)
## Medium-Risk AI Systems
-	Moderate potential impact if misused or failed
-	Examples: HR candidate screening, internal decision support with partial sensitive data
-	Approval: CAIO + CISO + DPO
## High-Risk AI Systems
-	Significant risk to health, safety, or fundamental rights
-	Examples: AI for loan approval, medical diagnostics, law enforcement
-	Approval: Full AI Governance Board and formal conformity assessment (EU AI Act Article 19)( Providers of high-risk AI systems are required to automatically generate and keep logs of system activity for at least six months. These logs must be maintained as long as the provider controls them and are intended to ensure transparency and accountability, especially for systems impacting health, safety, or fundamental rights.)
## Prohibited AI Systems
-	Systems forbidden by law or internal policy due to unacceptable risks
-	Examples: Social scoring, manipulative surveillance AI
-	Approval: Not allowed under any circumstances

# 3. Classification Process
**Initial Risk Assessment** by AI System Owner
<br />**Data Sensitivity Review** by Data Stewards
<br />**Technical Risk Assessment** by Security and AI Engineering
<br />**Ethical & Legal Review** by DPO / Legal
<br />**Final Risk Classification** by AI Governance Board
<br />Output includes a **Risk Assessment Report** and classification tags logged in the AI System Registry.
________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________
# 4. Integration with Approval Workflow
Risk classification directly determines the approval workflow tier, monitoring requirements, and reporting obligations. High-risk systems undergo additional documentation, conformity assessment, and governance board oversight. Medium- and low-risk systems follow proportionate controls and monitoring.
